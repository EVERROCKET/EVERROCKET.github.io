Work About

Topic Deltas

An analytics dashboard that helps investors discover market-moving insights from earnings calls.

The Challenge

Augmenting Human Expertise in Earnings Calls Analysis

The digital age has made huge volumes of data readily available to investors. To generate macro-level as well as company-specific insights in a timely manner, however, has been increasingly challenging due to the information overload in the financial markets.

If an investor were to go through all the earnings transcripts in the past 8 quarters of all the companies in the S&P 500 Index, it could take weeks to decipher the insightful information across different sectors, industries, and companies. Our goal was to create a tool that helps investors discover valuable investment opportunities from earnings transcripts in a more efficient and scalable way.

Monitoring 10 sectors and over 70 industries imposes a great cognitive burden for investors

My Role

As the lead designer on the team, I'm responsible for the user experience and visual design for the product from the ground up. I was involved in every aspect of the product development cycle, from conducting user research and usability testings, creating user flows and mockups, iterating testing prototypes, to delivering production-quality designs. I collaborated with 2 Product Managers, 3 Data Scientists, and 3 Software Engineers.

Research & Findings

Being the First to Discover Insights

User interviews were conducted with five domain experts in finance and investing to understand the workflow of earnings analysis. Our goals were to gain insights into the challenges they faced during the research process and what is important for making an investment decision. 

I asked structured questions while observing the experts conduct investment research. This allowed me to capture details of the workflows that would not have been elaborated in a conversational interview. Below are the insights that I found from the studies:

1

Scattered information adds a barrier to extracting insights

There are so many moving parts in making a trading decision that investors usually sit in front of multiple screens monitoring different information. This adds a significant barrier for investors to make a prompt and informed decision. 

2

Quantifying language at scale is almost impossible

Capturing the language nuances that move markets in earnings transcripts is almost impossible for investors to accomplish at scale. There are just too many transcripts to go through all across.

3

Significant topics always change from quarter to quarter

Depending on the company’s recent activities and trends in the markets, topics that have the most significant implications change over time. This dynamic nature makes it difficult to determine what’s important to follow each quarter. 

4

To be the first to discover is everything

Time is money and this is even truer in investing. Unless an investor has information that other competitors can't access easily, it’s a competition of being the earliest in finding profitable investment opportunities from the public data. 

Three key elements of making a good trading decision synthesized from the interviews

Ideation & Explorations

Converging User Needs into Ideas

Before jumping into designing a concrete solution, we wanted to explore different ideas of how we could approach the challenges. The question we asked ourselves was: If we were investors, what information we would want to be able to see? We started organizing different user stories based on the research insights.

Stories were then summarized into three categories:

  • Company: Information related to a company’s performance
  • Industry: Information related to an industry's performance
  • Topic: Information about a subject discussed by companies and industries

I used these as the three foundational components in the design phases later on.

Translating Unstructured Data into Insights

Early on, it was important to understand the technical feasibility and analytical possibilities of the data. We had a general sense that investors not only want to see all the numbers and metrics, but also the derived insights from unstructured data such as earnings transcripts. I worked with the Machine Learning Researchers to explore the data insights we were able to capture and how we could use them to help users with their problems. 

Targeting the user challenges with data insights, we proposed Topic Deltas, a product created to discover earnings nuanced insights. I proposed a couple of early versions of mockups that I tested with our team members and several customers.

Early mockup iterations

Discovery from Early Testings

With the early design iterations, we were able to identify some key insights into how users would use the product to find what they need in the investment process:

1

Context is important in the sentiment for a topic

Users found it difficult to trust the system on sentiment scoring without knowing the contexts. They usually needed to validate the scores by looking at the transcript snippets categorized to each topic. 

2

Sentiment changes from previous quarters matter more than the sentiments

Instead of using the sentiment itself, users were often looking for changes in sentiment from last quarter to predict stock performance. This is because the management from companies always tries to sound positive in earnings conferences.

3

Topics that are mentioned a lot might not necessarily be significant

We found that what was mentioned more frequently by the management team might not be considered as significant. Significant topics also depend on the trends happening around the markets, the company’s recent activities, and other factors.

These findings provided valuable guidance on the designs of the user flows, interactions, and data visualizations as we were iterating.

INTRODUCING TOPIC DELTAS

Discover Hidden Nuances that Move the Markets

Topic Deltas reads through earnings transcripts in a granular bottom-up way measuring topical sentiment changes across calls and between industry peers, giving investors the unique knowledge that scales their expertise.

Monitoring the Trends at a Glance

One of the biggest challenges for analysts or investors analyzing earnings has been tracking the macro trends across sectors, industries, and companies. With the three widgets on the top showing trending industries, companies, and topics based on sentiment changes, users can find what's trending from a macro-level perspective and quickly know where to pay attention to.

Capturing the dynamic change among sectors and industries as new calls come in was not easy. In the early iterations, we used a heat map where different shades of green and red represent positive and negative sentiment changes. After a couple of customer meetings and testings, we found that users cared more about which sectors or industries had more changes than others.

With a heat map, it was difficult to tell which ones had bigger changes from the colors as the scale of change in aggregate sentiment scores was very small. I decided to change it to a line chart as it captures more details about how the sentiment evolves over time as the earnings season is happening. This allows users to see more granular details about how different companies affect the sector and the industry's sentiment trend.

Turning Hours of Analysis into Minutes

Topic Deltas analyzes topical and aggregate sentiments for each company right after the earnings call. Users can gain an edge by finding out what companies and topics have the most sentiment changes from the last quarter as soon as the company announces.

As the research showed, investors rely more on sentiment delta than sentiment itself for predicting a company's stock performance. Along with the top three trending topics that have the most sentiment delta, these two elements allow users to see which companies that have just reported are worth more attention at a glance.

All You Want to Know in One Place

Topic Deltas identifies key themes by comparing and contrasting granular topics over time and against those referenced by industry peers. What's driving the industry markets? How the industry has evolved over time? Who has under or outperformed? Users can find all of the important information in one place.

In early iterations, users needed to select a sector and industry before they could see any data. They would then see an aggregate sentiment and importance score history for the companies selected. We learned from the testings that this took users too much effort to compare performances across companies.

This was changed to a flow where users start with selecting an industry and seeing all the relevant information within the industry after. I also put more focus on the sentiment delta so it's easier for users to find actionable insights like out or underperformers.

Flexibility to Dig Deeper

Topic Deltas covers all the topical sentiments within an industry and allows users to dig deeper into the sentiment history in the past quarters, for every topic, every company.

Previously, we provided filtering options for users to select topics under different thresholds. We later found that it wasn't used as much as expected. Users wanted to have a more holistic view before they decided to dive deeper. This resulted in a simpler user flow and layout that adapted to the users' workflow.

A Glassbox that Provides Transparency

In the Earnings Calls page, users can compare and contrast the key statements in the call classified by different topics as well as the historical performance for each topic. The semantic details of textured, nuanced language not only provide the transparency of how the machine works behind the scenes but also enable investors to find patterns that were previously invisible.

In earlier versions, the Earnings Transcript page showed the topical snippets from the two most recent quarters side by side. Users needed to scroll to see all the topics. Some topics might have a lot more content than others, which would take a huge portion of the screen and make the browsing cumbersome. We also had feedback that the big boxes that were color-coded for sentiment scoring were sometimes distracting when reading the texts.

I redesigned it to a layout where users can navigate between topics with the topic section on the left and the topical snippets on the right. This gives users more space to read through the content for each topic while spending less time navigating.

REFLECTIONS

Making Design Decisions Through Research

Designing a product for a domain often requires a significant amount of background knowledge. This also means that some of the design principles that I learned from past experiences might not apply in certain case scenarios. In the beginning, I tended to stuck myself in the thought process and made decisions based on unvalidated assumptions.

It did not take long for me to realize that research is the key to understanding user behaviors and problems and validating design assumptions and ideas at an early stage. I was fortunate to have had the opportunity to work with a group of knowledgable and supportive domain experts and users during the process. Through research, I was able to obtain insights into the user space and iterated my designs in the right direction.
